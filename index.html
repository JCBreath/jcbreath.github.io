Project Description

Urban heat islands (UHI) can intensify temperatures by 0.4 to 11 °C, especially at night, increasing health risks such as mortality and morbidity, with the elderly and low-income individuals being most susceptible. In the U.S., rising temperatures may escalate heat-related health issues among these vulnerable groups. The issue is compounded by heatwaves that exacerbate urban heat stress. Accurate city microclimate monitoring remains crucial for enhancing urban climate resilience. This initiative leverages the Notre Dame Campus as a research platform, combining new sensor technologies, spatial analytics, and machine learning to analyze how urban structures affect microclimates and heat stress. It represents a joint effort between the School of Architecture, the College of Engineering, and the Offices of Sustainability and Facility Management.

Ground Mobile Data Collection

First-round data collection

On August 20th, 2023, the research team collected data. Throughout the day, temperature data were gathered, with the day divided into three distinct time slots: morning, afternoon, and evening. The lowest temperature on this date is measures as 66.2 ºF @5:05 AM and the highest temperature was 89.6 ºF @5:10 PM. The average temperature and humidity were captured as 77 ºF and 74.85%, respectively. Total of 6 person were contributed in the data collecting and each 2 people formed a group.

Starting from 7 am, teams utilized Tracki GPS devices for precise location tracking and Elitech temperature sensors to log data with an accuracy of ±0.5℃. Maps were provided for targeted data collection areas. From 12:30 pm, efforts intensified to capture heat island effects, with two additional teams recording perceived and environmental temperatures. Teams stayed stationary for minutes to ensure accurate readings, while others moved to measure real-feel temperatures. The aim was to compare data to understand the impact of heat islands on user experience. The evening session, starting at 5:45 pm, replicated the morning's methodology, concluding with device collection for data integration and maintenance.

Data processing and analysis

As we process the data gathered during the initial walk, we refine 1484 rows of entries into 931 rows of more accurate and reliable data points.

Second-round data collection

On October 29th, 2023, the research team conducted 2nd-round data collection. . The lowest temperature on this date is measured as 42.8ºF and the highest temperature was 48.2ºF. The average temperature was captured as 44.77 ºF. A total of 5 people contributed to the data collecting and each individual captured the data of a certain area in the campus. 14 routes were defined on Google map and the campus was divided between the data collection members (see Figure 1). The goal was to capture the data for every time slot in less than 1 hour to avoid any sort of drastic weather changes.

Rooftop Thermal Image

A thermal image, often called a thermographic image or thermogram, is a visual representation of temperature variations in an object or a scene.

On October 17, 2023, the University of Notre Dame facility management team flew the drone carrying the thermal camera and separately captured thermal images of ten buildings on campus. The weather station (at the airport) showed a temperature range of 42 to 63 degrees Fahrenheit.

Roof temperature analysis

Figure 1 demonstrates that the "O'Neill" and "DeBartolo" buildings display a tighter range of thermal intensities, suggesting more uniform temperatures. In contrast, the "Fizpatrick Hall" and "Engineering North" rooftops exhibit a wider range of thermal intensities, indicating larger temperature variations. We also observe that the "Facilities Building" has the highest overall thermal intensities compared to the other buildings, implying the hottest surface temperature on its rooftop.
